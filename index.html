About

Welcome

I am a linguist at Boston University working on Language Documentation, Historical Phonology, & Prosody. I'm broadly interested in prosodic typology, the phonetics and phonology of tone and intonation, and the ways that historical changes might inform our models of grammar and underlying representations. Most of my research focuses on the documentation of Northern Pomo, a dormant Indigenous language of Northern California, where I have found evidence for an emerging lexical tone contrast! My dissertation focuses on reconstructing the historical processes through which this contrast developed over time. Because Northern Pomo is dormant, much of this work involves phonetic analysis of archived audio materials — a process I try to conduct in dialogue with ongoing community-led revitalization efforts by building tools that make these recordings more accessible.

Before joining Boston University, I completed my Bachelor's degree at James Madison University where I worked in Dr. Kit Ying (Vivien) Chan's Psycholinguistics Lab where I helped design and run experiments studying the effects of foreign accented speech on short and long term memory encoding. I am currently a 5th year PhD student who has recently advanced to doctoral candidacy in the Department of Linguistics.
